Popular Recipes in 19th Century Confectionary

Confectionary Word Cloud

In order to know the most popular confectionery
food in 19th century, we have built a database
with 2037 recipes from year 1772 to 1862. The
database has been analyzed to generate this
confectionary word cloud.

In these recipes, "Ice" is absolutely the top  word
in confectionery recipes, followed by cream,
water, biscuit, syrup, cake and orange. The word
frequency statistics could roughly reflect popolar
 confectioneries and common ingredients in that
time period. 

1. Ice: 263

2. Cream: 179

3. Water: 136

4. Biscuit: 120

5. Syrup: 116

6. Cake: 114

Ice with Fruits

"Ice" is no doubt the most popular word
according to our word cloud. Ice confectionery
wasn't formalized as recipe in UK cookery books
until the 18th century. Ice cream was then
brought to average people and became very
popular street snack in 1840s. 

We have generate a new word cloud by counting
all the recipes with "ice" and found out that fruit
flavours are significantly more popular than the
others. Among them, strawberry and raspberry
flavours are the most popular ones. 

Raspberry Ice Cream(The confectioners' hand-book and practical guide to the art of sugar boiling [1883])
Process:

  • Rub the raspberries through a fine hair sieve
  • Add a pint of this pulp to a quart of the custard as above
  • Pour it in the freezer
  • Color with a little cochineal and freeze

Currant Water Ice (The complete confectioner[1808])

Process:

  • Take a large spoonful and a half of currant jelly
  • Put it into a bason
  • Squeeze two lemons
  • Add half a gill of syrup and a pint of water
  • Freeze it rich.

Grape Water Ice (The complete confectioner[1808])

Process:

  • Take two handsful of elder flowers, put them into a pot
  • Boil a pint of water and pour it over them, cover them close
  • Take two gills of syrup and the juice of three lemons
  • Drain all the water from the flowers, add it to the rest, making it palatable, pass it and freeze it
  • When it is frozen, put it in the shape of a bunch of grapes
  • Close it well and cover the mould with half a sheet of paper
  • Put it into the ice and salt for one hour before you tum it out

Flavoured Fruit Syrup

Flavoured syrup is widely used in confectionery for making flavoured drinks, creating desserts or adding sweetness and depth of flavour to cocktails. It could be intended from the analysis results of the 19th century confectionery database that, fruit flavour was quite popular in the time period. Orange syrup has biggest fans, followed by currant, lemon and pine. 

Orange Syrup (The Housekeeper's Assistant [1836])

Process:

  • Take eight China oranges and rasp them in a basin
  • Squeeze fourteen lernons with the rinds, mix them together in a basin with a spoon
  • Strain all the juice through a fine lawn sieve;
  • Take a quart of syrup, and boil it almost to a caramel
  • Add the juice to the syrup
  • Put it in your bottles, and cork and bladder them the next day

Lemon Syrup (The Housekeeper's Assistant [1836])

Process:

  • Take a pint of lemon juice
  • Strain it through a sieve
  • Boil two pints of syrup
  • Add the juice
  • Put it on the fire
  • Let it boil to a pearl
  • Skim it carefully
  • When cold, bottle it

Red Currant Syrup (The Royal English and Foreign Confectioner [1862])


Ingredients:

  • 1 lb of sugar made into syrup of 35 degrees strength (tried with the saccharometer, or syrup gauge)
  • 3 gills of red currant juice strained through a hair sieve.

Process:

  • Mix these ingredients cold
  • fill the bottles, cork and tie clown
  • six minutes' gentle ebullition, the bottles to be put on in cold water
  • when cold, dip the nozles of the bottles in bottle wax
  • stack them in the usual manner in bins, in a cold cellar

Biscuits

Biscuit is another commonly mentioned pastery term in the database. Elizabeth David has argued about the modern-day difference in the English language regarding the term "biscuit" in her English Bread and Yeast Cookery that: 

"It is interesting that these soft biscuits (such as scones) are common to Scortland and Guernsey,  and that the term biscuit as applied to a soft product was retained in these places, and in America, whereas in England it has completely died out."

It is unexpected that in the 19th century database, the most common word appears with biscuit is still "ice", followed by orange, savoy and cream. It seems 19th century people tend to make and enjoy biscuits freezingly.   

Iced Biscuits (The Royal English and Foreign Confectioner [1862])

Ingredients:

  • 1 pint of syrup of 32 degrees strength
  • 1 pint of strawberry pulp
  • 15 yolks of eggs, 1 oz. of vanilla sugar
  • 8 pint of double cream whipped

Process:

  • Mix the syrup and yolks, strain
  • Add the strawberry pulp and vanilla sugar
  • Set the composition
  • Incorporate the whipped cream lightly and fill the paper cases (either plaited and circular or square)
  • These must be surrounded each witha band of stiff paper of sufficient width to reach half an inch above the edges of the cases
  • The bands to be pinched, stuck, or pinned together at one corner, so as to render them secure.
  • The biscuits Filled, place them in the ice cave, and imbed this in ice in the usual way.
  • When about to send these biscuits to table, after having first removed the bands of paper, cover their surfaces with brown coloured ratafias bruised to a fine powder and sifted upon them.
  • It will be obvious that the bands of paper to be placed round the cases are intended to give the biscuits the appearance of the composition having risen out of the cases, while the biscuits are supposed to have been baked.

To Make Orange Biscuit (The complete confectioner[1772])

Process:

  • Take your oranges and water them two days
  • Boil them tender
  • Shift the water they are boiled in
  • Put them to another that is hot
  • When they are tender take them up
  • Put them in a cloth to dry (minding the meat be taken out of the oranges)
  • Take their weight and half of double-refined sugar, finely beaten
  • Let your oranges be beat in a stone mortar
  • Strew your sugal on them as they are beating
  • When the pulp is very fall, and the sugal taken up with beating
  • Take it out and lay it on glaffes like your paste (minding to be quick in laying it out for fear it grows rough and dries too fait)
  • Fet them in an oven after manchets
  • keep them in a stove to dry 3 beat the pulp of your oranges very fall, or else they will look rough, dark, tough, and harsh.

How to Make Savoy Biscuits (The complete confectioner[1772]

Process:

  • Take eight eggs, seperate the whites from the yolks
  • Beat your whites till they are very high
  • Put our yolks in with a pound of sugar
  • Beat this for a quarter of an hour
  • When your oven is ready, put in one pound of fine flour
  • Stir it till it is well mixed
  • Lay your biscuits upon the paper and ice them (only taking care your oven is hot enough to bake them speedily)
