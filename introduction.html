Introduction: David, Confectionery, and Others

About the Bequest

Elizabeth David was a popular British cookery writer who strongly influenced UK with her articles and books in cookery and food culture. 

David willed her pre-1800 cookery books collection to the Warburg Institute Library after her death in 1992. Now, the library holds approximately 233 books that formerly belonged to David on subjects ranging from histories of food culture, cookery and baking manuals from France, Italy and Britain, and guides to inns and taverns. There are also books that reflect David's interests in other fields, such as past life, witchcraft, cabinet and journey records. 

Approximately 120 of the bequest books are now out of copyright, so their contents and illustrations could be used in exhibition. 

Confectionery Or Confectionary

Pastries, cakes, candied nuts, biscuits, chocolates, bubble gum... Although confectionery is not Elizabeth David's main food interest, it does have a core status in European food culture. 

Before the Industrial Revolution, sweets and cakes came with high price as a luxury delicacy available only to wealthy. In the 19th century, sweet treats started to be mass-produced through mechanisation. Confectionery price dropped sharply and availability grew to more audience. The David's confectionery books from 1770s to 1860s collection see this revolution. 

This exhibition will look into the confectionery culture in 90 years from 1770s, use digital text analysis tools and multimedia presentation modes, to represent a prosperous confectionery culture from 18th to 19th century.  

There is an argument in the suffix of "confectionery". People use "confectionery" more commonly today to describe food items with rich sugar and carbohydrates. However, most Elizabeth David Bequest books use "confectionary" instead. 

According to Collins English Dictionary, "confectionery" and "confectionary" have slight differences. "Confectionery" refers to "sweets and chocolates" and "the art or business of a confectioner", while "confectionary" refers to "a place where confections are kept or made". 

In most David Bequest collections, "confectionery" and "confectionary" share the same meaning of "art of making confections". There are "a practical treatise on the art of confectionary in all its branches", "the whole art of confectionary made easy" and "Ornamental confectionery and the art of baking". 

"Confectionery" is used in this exhibition for more commonly acceptance.
